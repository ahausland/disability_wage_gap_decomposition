# Disability Wage Gap Decomposition

Using American Community Survey data from 2015 to 2024, this project analyzes the persistence of the disability wage gap in full-time working adults. Linear regression models and Oaxaca decompositions were performed to evaluate differences in both observed characteristics and returns to those characteristics between disabled and non-disabled workers. Analyses were separate by gender, which allowed for closer examination of the intersection between gender and disability. The results show that the disability wage gap remains substantial in men and women over the sample period. A large majority of the gap is unexplained by observable characteristics. The regression results fruther indicate that disabled workers experience lower returns to key factors such as education, age, and work from home status. While education acconts for the largest share of the explained component, its contribution declines over time. In contrast, the role of working from home increases sharply during 2020-2021, reflecting the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. However, the WFH component diminishes in subsequent years. The findings in this project suggest that the disability wage gap is driven by both differences in characteristics and significantly lower reutnrs to those characteristics, which may reflect potential discrimination.

See the [full report](/econ_final_report.pdf) for further details about the project. The [R code](https://github.com/ahausland/disability_wage_gap_decomposition/disability_wage_gap.html) is also viewable.
